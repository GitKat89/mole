Information about moles, melanomas and how they are classified

Moles

Moles, liver spots, beauty patches or nevis are terms for visible, circumscribed, chronic lesions of the skin. The vast majority of moles are harmless, but a small proportion are melanomas. Melanomas are malignant tumors, which should be removed as early as possible. Most melanomas are newly emerging moles. Only 25% of melanomas originate from older, originally harmless moles. In order to distinguish harmless moles from melanomas, dermatologists perform skin cancer screenings.

Skin Cancer Screening

In skin cancer screening, dermatologists try to distinguish benign from malignant moles. They usually use dermatoscopes to be able to recognize the structure of the moles more precisely. Dermatoscopes are optical devices that provide optimal illumination and magnification. Often moles and melanomas cannot be clearly distinguished from each other, so that a tissue removal with subsequent examination is carried out.

Further Information


The mole classifier uses a convolutional neural network with the so-called ResNet50 architecture. The model was trained with labeled image data of the ISIC Challenge XXX (add link). The dataset consists of 2169 melanomas and 18,566 atypical nevi (as of 17 October 2018). By training with this data, the model has learned to distinguish and classify different characteristics of benign moles and melanomas.

In a recent paper (add link), artificial neural networks outperform even chief dermatologists. However, it must be mentioned that the dermatologists made the diagnosis on the basis of digital images. The images were taken with dermatoscopes that eliminate surface reflection. Uploading images of your moles taken with a smartphone or a camera will not reach the same performance as using images using dermatoscopes.

The network shows the result in a percentage probability distribution for the different classes. Together the respective probabilities always add up to 100 %. This means that the model also generates ( senseless ) results for uploaded images that do not contain a mole at all. Furthermore, the network is influenced by conspicuous areas outside the network. Therefore, uploaded images should preferably only show the birthmark and the surrounding skin area.
